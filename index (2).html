Prototype Design Report

IV Drip Optical Alert System

A non-contact sensor device that uses light refraction to detect when an IV glucose bag is nearly empty — and automatically alerts nursing staff.

IR Sensor Laser Diode Refraction of Light CBSE Class 10 Physics Medical IoT

Abstract

Intravenous (IV) drips are widely used in hospitals to deliver fluids, glucose, and medication directly into a patient's bloodstream. A critical problem in clinical settings is that when the IV bag empties completely, air can enter the patient's vein — a serious and potentially fatal complication. Nurses cannot constantly monitor every patient's drip.

This prototype proposes a low-cost, non-invasive optical device that clips onto the IV tubing. It uses a laser emitter and an IR (infrared) photodiode sensor on opposite sides of a transparent section of tubing. When liquid is present, the laser refracts inside the fluid and does not reach the sensor. When the liquid drops below the sensor level, the laser beam travels straight across and strikes the sensor — triggering a buzzer or wireless alert to nursing staff.

Scientific Principle

Refraction of Light — CBSE Class 10, Chapter: Light (Reflection and Refraction)

When a ray of light travels from one medium (e.g. air) into another medium of different optical density (e.g. glucose solution), it bends at the boundary. This bending is called refraction.

The degree of bending is described by Snell's Law:
n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂

Glucose solution has a higher refractive index (~1.36–1.38) than air (~1.00). When the laser enters the liquid, it bends significantly — enough that it misses the IR sensor on the other side. When air replaces the liquid, no refraction occurs: the beam travels straight and hits the sensor.

📘 CBSE Class 10 Concepts Used

  • Refraction of light — bending of light at a medium boundary (Chapter: Light – Reflection and Refraction)
  • Refractive index — ratio of speed of light in vacuum to speed in a medium; n = c/v
  • Snell's Law — n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂
  • Total Internal Reflection (related concept) — at critical angles, light can be totally reflected inside a denser medium
  • Photoelectric effect / light sensors — IR photodiodes respond to incident light (Class 10 & 12 boundary topic)

How the Prototype is Made

1

Design the Clip Housing

Using a 3D modeling tool (TinkerCAD or Fusion 360), design a small C-shaped clip that can snap around standard IV tubing. The two arms of the clip hold the laser diode on one side and the IR photodiode on the exact opposite side, both aligned at the same height.

2

Mount Laser and IR Sensor

Solder the 650nm laser diode into one arm and the IR photodiode into the other. Connect them to the Arduino via thin insulated wires. Add a 100Ω resistor in series with the laser to limit current.

3

Write the Arduino Code

Program the Arduino to continuously read the photodiode's analog output. When the sensor voltage crosses a threshold (beam detected = air in tube), the code triggers the buzzer and sends an HTTP request via the Wi-Fi module to a nurse-facing dashboard or mobile notification.

4

Calibrate the Sensor Height

Position the clip at roughly 10–15% of the total fluid level from the bottom — early enough to warn nurses with time to respond, but not so early that it triggers false alarms. Perform calibration tests with water and glucose solution to confirm refraction is sufficient to block the beam.

5

Test and Validate

Fill a transparent IV tube with glucose solution and gradually drain it. Confirm: (a) no trigger while liquid is above sensor level; (b) consistent trigger when liquid falls below. Measure false-positive rate and response latency (should be <1 second).

Limitations & Future Scope

The current prototype is a proof-of-concept. The following limitations should be addressed before clinical deployment:

  • Bubble interference: Air bubbles in the fluid could cause false triggers. A debounce delay (e.g. 3-second sustained signal) in the code mitigates this.
  • Tube opacity: Works best with transparent/translucent tubing. Opaque tubing would require a different sensor approach.
  • Sterility: The clip is external and does not contact the fluid — but must still be sterilizable (autoclave-safe materials).
  • Regulatory approval: Any clinical deployment requires approval from medical device authorities (e.g. CDSCO in India, FDA in USA).

Future enhancements could include: flow rate measurement, multi-bag monitoring via a central dashboard, and integration with hospital Electronic Health Record (EHR) systems.
